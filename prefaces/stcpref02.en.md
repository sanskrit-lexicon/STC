# FOREWORD

There hardly seems to be any need to justify at length the publication of a Sanskrit–French dictionary, more than a hundred years after Indian studies began to be cultivated actively in our country. It is, rather, surprising to observe that until now people have been able to make do with the *Dictionnaire classique sanscrit-français* of Émile Burnouf and L. Leupol (Paris, 1866), a work as poor as it is inaccurate, unusable today and doubtless outdated from its very birth. There do, assuredly, exist excellent dictionaries in English and German, but it goes without saying that one cannot require of all those who take up Sanskrit studies a sufficient command of those two languages, and even where they did possess them, it is impractical and sometimes dangerous to have to render each notion by two equivalents, through which it loses something of its force and precision.

The present work naturally owes the essential part of its materials to the two Petersburg Dictionaries, which will dominate Sanskrit lexicography for a long time yet. It is not, however, a mere extract from them. In the first place, use has been made of the more recent — sometimes personal — information contained in the Dictionaries of Monier-Williams and of Apte; as well as of the useful « Nachträge » compiled by R. Schmidt (1). Then, and above all, in every case where it seemed necessary, the authenticity of a form or of a meaning has been verified in the texts themselves, and where needed in the commentators; an effort has been made to supply throughout a precise translation, one that is not a mere tracing of an English or German rendering. In particular, information of a geographical order, and technical terms, have been revised. It is also right to mention that one of the collaborators, Mme Stchoupak, supplied for this book the slips of a complete lexicon of Bhavabhûti which she had long had under way.

Given that this Dictionary is of a strictly elementary character, and given on the other hand that the excerpting already carried out is extremely uneven, it was thought best to confine ourselves to the words and meanings that occur in a determined selection of texts: texts that carry authority, whether for their literary value or for the practical introduction they afford to those who take up the reading of Sanskrit. Moreover, these texts have been chosen so as to represent the various periods and the various styles with sufficient breadth.

(1) Incidentally, the « Concordance » of Jacob and the Index of proper names of the MhBh. by Sörensen.
