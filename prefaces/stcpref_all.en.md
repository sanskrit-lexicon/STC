## Page 02 — Preface, 1 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [stchou-vor0005.jpg](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/stcpref/stcpref02.html)</sub>

There hardly seems to be any need to justify at length the publication of a Sanskrit–French dictionary, more than a hundred years after Indian studies began to be cultivated actively in our country. It is, rather, surprising to observe that until now people have been able to make do with the *Dictionnaire classique sanscrit-français* of Émile Burnouf and L. Leupol (Paris, 1866), a work as poor as it is inaccurate, unusable today and doubtless outdated from its very birth. There do, assuredly, exist excellent dictionaries in English and German, but it goes without saying that one cannot require of all those who take up Sanskrit studies a sufficient command of those two languages, and even where they did possess them, it is impractical and sometimes dangerous to have to render each notion by two equivalents, through which it loses something of its force and precision.

The present work naturally owes the essential part of its materials to the two Petersburg Dictionaries, which will dominate Sanskrit lexicography for a long time yet. It is not, however, a mere extract from them. In the first place, use has been made of the more recent — sometimes personal — information contained in the Dictionaries of Monier-Williams and of Apte; as well as of the useful « Nachträge » compiled by R. Schmidt (1). Then, and above all, in every case where it seemed necessary, the authenticity of a form or of a meaning has been verified in the texts themselves, and where needed in the commentators; an effort has been made to supply throughout a precise translation, one that is not a mere tracing of an English or German rendering. In particular, information of a geographical order, and technical terms, have been revised. It is also right to mention that one of the collaborators, Mme Stchoupak, supplied for this book the slips of a complete lexicon of Bhavabhûti which she had long had under way.

Given that this Dictionary is of a strictly elementary character, and given on the other hand that the excerpting already carried out is extremely uneven, it was thought best to confine ourselves to the words and meanings that occur in a determined selection of texts: texts that carry authority, whether for their literary value or for the practical introduction they afford to those who take up the reading of Sanskrit. Moreover, these texts have been chosen so as to represent the various periods and the various styles with sufficient breadth.

----

(1) Incidentally, the « Concordance » of Jacob and the Index of proper names of the MhBh. by Sörensen.

## Page 03 — Preface, 2 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [stchou-vor0007.jpg](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/stcpref/stcpref03.html)</sub>

[page II]

Properly Vedic literature had to be excluded altogether; likewise, for quite different reasons, Buddhist literature.

The following were thus worked up:

For the Upaniṣad, the group of the great Upaniṣad (Īçā Aitareya Kaṭha Kena Chāndogya Taittirīya Praçna Bṛhadāraṇyaka Māṇḍūkya Muṇḍaka Çvetāçvatara);

For the epic language, the Mahābhārata and the Rāmāyaṇa;

For the Dharmaçāstra, the Manusmṛti;

For the Purāṇa, the Bhāgavatapurāṇa;

For the language of tales, the Pañcatantra, the Hitopadeça, the Kathāsaritsāgara, etc.;

For dramatic literature, the comedies of Kālidāsa, of Bhavabhūti, the Mudrārākṣasa, the Ratnāvalī, the Mṛcchakaṭika, the Veṇīsaṃhāra;

For the novel, Daṇḍin, Bāṇa and Subandhu;

For lyric and gnomic poetry, Kālidāsa, Māgha, Bhāravi, Bhartṛhari, Amaru and, in general, the stanzas collected in the « Indische Sprüche » of Böhtlingk.

For the vocabulary thus delimited a wide choice of derivatives and compounds has been given; no claim is made to completeness; it is hoped at least that nothing important has been omitted. Finally, we have not refrained from incorporating some interesting technical term (e.g. of the grammatical or philosophical language) that did not occur in these texts.

Alphabetical order is broken only as regards nominal compounds and (in part) derivatives: to save space and to bring out the relationship of the forms, compounds (except those having an independent bearing) have been grouped under the first member and distinguished typographically from simple words: first come those whose first member reproduces exactly the form of the word that heads the article; then those in which the final of that member is modified by sandhi; finally the juxtaposed ones. Thus, under *kathā-*, one will find first compounds of the type *kathā-krama-*, *kathā-chala-*, etc.; then those in which the boundary between the two members is masked by a contraction, *kathārambha-*, etc.; under *ātman-*, first those in *ātma°*, then the types *ātmādhika-… ātmecchā-*, etc.; finally the juxtaposed ones in *ātmaṃ° ātmanā° ātmī°*.

In an analogous way, derivatives have often been gathered under the word that serves as their base: constantly the abstracts in *-tā- -tva-*, the comparatives in *-tara- -tama-*; ordinarily, the derivatives in *-ka- -vant-*, etc.; no fixed rule has been adopted; the twofold principle has been observed of remaining clear and of tightening the redaction. The procedure has been extended and generalised to formations with preverb, where one always has the leisure of referring, for further detail, to the corresponding simple formations.

For the rest we have sought to be of service to beginners by multiplying cross-references, by recalling in all useful cases the form of the root, by enumerating the principal tenses of the verbs (1), the noteworthy grammatical forms (2), etc.

----

(1) First the tenses of conjugation proper (present, perfect, aorist, future); the derived conjugation (passive, causative, desiderative, intensive); the nominal conjugation (adjective in *-ta-*, infinitive, absolutive). We have relied here on the excellent repertory of the *Roots* of Whitney. — For verbs with preverbs, look for these tenses under the simple verb.

(2) The feminine (of adjectives) is indicated in all difficult cases, in particular the feminine in *-ī-* for themes in *-a-*.

## Page 05 — Preface, 4 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [stchou-vor0009.jpg](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/stcpref/stcpref05.html)</sub>

[page IV]

The sign ° before or after a word means that this word serves as the last or the first member of a compound (1).

For derivatives cited under the base word, only the suffix is generally written, thus:

> (*ūrdhva-*) … *-kam* : read *ūrdhvakam*.

However, if the base theme ends in a vowel and the suffix likewise begins with a vowel (or with a *y-*), the vowel of the suffix is substituted for that of the theme, thus:

> (*ūrdhva-*) … *-am* : read *ūrdhvam*

or

> (*kumāra-*) … *-ikā-* : read *kumārikā-*.

It seemed harmless to set down simply *-ikā-* even where this suffix is to be not added to the preceding theme (in *-aka-*) but substituted for the element *-aka-*, thus:

> (*putraka-*) … *-ikā-* : read *putrikā-*.

and, analogously, (*kālavant-*) … *-vatī-*, read *kālavatī-*; or, in verbal forms, *ṛcchati -te* : read *ṛcchati ṛcchate* (2).

The gender of a word is not repeated when it agrees with that of the word immediately preceding, within the same article.

The note « f(eminine) » is not specially indicated for adjectives: consequently, the group

> « *uru- -(v)ī- a.* » is to be understood as (adjective) *uru-*, feminine *urvī-*.

\*\*\*

The present work has been divided among each collaborator into three portions of roughly equal extent; each of them, first worked up independently, was then reviewed by the three authors both on the manuscript and on the proofs. It is hoped that the discordances, hard to avoid in a work of this nature, have thereby been reduced to a minimum.

This work could not be brought to a close without tacitly rendering homage to the revered memory of M. Senart: M. Senart had ardently wished for the publication of a Sanskrit–French dictionary; he held it to be an indispensable working tool for the progress of our studies and the success of our teaching. He even indirectly favoured its publication. Indeed, out of the legacy with which he had benefited the Académie des Inscriptions et Belles-Lettres, that institution kindly drew three arrears, which facilitated the preparation of the Dictionary. We wish here to express our keen gratitude to the Académie des Inscriptions, and in particular to M. A. Foucher, who in this matter has been a precious support to us.
