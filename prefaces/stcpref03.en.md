# FOREWORD

[page II]

Properly Vedic literature had to be excluded altogether; likewise, for quite different reasons, Buddhist literature.

The following were thus worked up:

For the Upaniṣad, the group of the great Upaniṣad (Īçā Aitareya Kaṭha Kena Chāndogya Taittirīya Praçna Bṛhadāraṇyaka Māṇḍūkya Muṇḍaka Çvetāçvatara);

For the epic language, the Mahābhārata and the Rāmāyaṇa;

For the Dharmaçāstra, the Manusmṛti;

For the Purāṇa, the Bhāgavatapurāṇa;

For the language of tales, the Pañcatantra, the Hitopadeça, the Kathāsaritsāgara, etc.;

For dramatic literature, the comedies of Kālidāsa, of Bhavabhūti, the Mudrārākṣasa, the Ratnāvalī, the Mṛcchakaṭika, the Veṇīsaṃhāra;

For the novel, Daṇḍin, Bāṇa and Subandhu;

For lyric and gnomic poetry, Kālidāsa, Māgha, Bhāravi, Bhartṛhari, Amaru and, in general, the stanzas collected in the « Indische Sprüche » of Böhtlingk.

For the vocabulary thus delimited a wide choice of derivatives and compounds has been given; no claim is made to completeness; it is hoped at least that nothing important has been omitted. Finally, we have not refrained from incorporating some interesting technical term (e.g. of the grammatical or philosophical language) that did not occur in these texts.

Alphabetical order is broken only as regards nominal compounds and (in part) derivatives: to save space and to bring out the relationship of the forms, compounds (except those having an independent bearing) have been grouped under the first member and distinguished typographically from simple words: first come those whose first member reproduces exactly the form of the word that heads the article; then those in which the final of that member is modified by sandhi; finally the juxtaposed ones. Thus, under *kathā-*, one will find first compounds of the type *kathā-krama-*, *kathā-chala-*, etc.; then those in which the boundary between the two members is masked by a contraction, *kathārambha-*, etc.; under *ātman-*, first those in *ātma°*, then the types *ātmādhika-… ātmecchā-*, etc.; finally the juxtaposed ones in *ātmaṃ° ātmanā° ātmī°*.

In an analogous way, derivatives have often been gathered under the word that serves as their base: constantly the abstracts in *-tā- -tva-*, the comparatives in *-tara- -tama-*; ordinarily, the derivatives in *-ka- -vant-*, etc.; no fixed rule has been adopted; the twofold principle has been observed of remaining clear and of tightening the redaction. The procedure has been extended and generalised to formations with preverb, where one always has the leisure of referring, for further detail, to the corresponding simple formations.

For the rest we have sought to be of service to beginners by multiplying cross-references, by recalling in all useful cases the form of the root, by enumerating the principal tenses of the verbs (1), the noteworthy grammatical forms (2), etc.

----

(1) First the tenses of conjugation proper (present, perfect, aorist, future); the derived conjugation (passive, causative, desiderative, intensive); the nominal conjugation (adjective in *-ta-*, infinitive, absolutive). We have relied here on the excellent repertory of the *Roots* of Whitney. — For verbs with preverbs, look for these tenses under the simple verb.

(2) The feminine (of adjectives) is indicated in all difficult cases, in particular the feminine in *-ī-* for themes in *-a-*.
